# Soul Reflection Design Guide

## Purpose

This document defines the architecture and principles behind the Soul Reflection feature in the Isaiah Study App. It is a companion to the Homiletic Style Guide and should be read alongside it. Where the Homiletic Style Guide governs *how* individual insights are written, this document governs *how the entire soul reflection is designed* — scenario, options, and insights as an integrated system.

---

## The Core Philosophy: Body-First, Life-Situation

The soul reflection is not a comprehension question. It is not "What did this passage mean to you?" It is not a prompt for spiritual journaling. It is a structured encounter that begins in the body and the world before it names anything theological.

**The progression is always:**

1. A real-life situation the reader inhabits physically (not spiritually)
2. A visceral response the reader recognizes in their body before their mind categorizes it
3. An insight that enters that response and accompanies it somewhere

**What this prevents:**
- The reader performing spirituality rather than practicing it
- The reader skipping their actual emotional state to produce the "correct" theological response
- The app functioning as a spiritual performance metric rather than a formation tool

---

## Anatomy of a Soul Reflection

Each soul reflection has three components:

### 1. The Scenario

**What it is:** A concrete, specific life situation placed in the present tense that creates a physical and emotional context for the reader before any theological category is named.

**Governing principles:**

- **Embodied specificity.** The scenario has a location, a time of day, a physical sensation, or a specific relationship. "You are sitting across from your oldest friend at a kitchen table. It is late. The wine is gone." Not: "You are in a difficult relational situation."

- **Emotionally complex.** The scenario should not have a clear "right" response — it should create genuine ambiguity that makes all 7 options plausible. A good scenario is one where a mature, thoughtful person could credibly feel any of the 7 options.

- **Thematically calibrated.** The scenario corresponds to the theological/spiritual content of the section — but obliquely, not directly. The reader should not be able to name the correspondence immediately. The connection should arrive during the insight, not during the scenario.

- **Not about church.** Or at least, not always. The majority of scenarios should be set in ordinary life: a kitchen table, a grocery store, an empty classroom, a hospital waiting room, a car in the dark. The spiritual life is lived in these places, not only in the sanctuary.

- **Past tense or present action.** The scenario sets the stage in the past ("You have just received a call") or drops the reader into ongoing action ("You are sitting"). Avoid future hypotheticals ("Imagine that you...") — they create distance rather than presence.

**Length:** 3-6 sentences. Long enough to establish specificity; short enough to feel like a door, not a lecture.

---

### 2. The Options

**What they are:** Seven 1-2 sentence visceral, body-first responses that a real person might have in the scenario described. They are written in first person, present tense, as though the reader is speaking from inside the response.

**Governing principles:**

- **Body before mind.** The options begin in the body — jaw, hands, chest, stomach, throat, eyes — before any mental or verbal processing. "My hands start shaking" before "I feel afraid." This is not metaphor; it is physiologically accurate. The body knows before the mind names.

- **Emotional breadth.** Across 7 options, the full emotional vocabulary should be represented. The palette includes but is not limited to: rage, relief, numbness, shame, tenderness, disgust, awe, dread, longing, grief, confusion, bitter humor, protective instinct, surrender, defiance. No emotional register should be implied to be more spiritually mature than another.

- **Equal dignity in the writing.** The option that the writer is most sympathetic to must receive no more literary care than the option the writer finds most difficult. The numbness option must be written as precisely as the tenderness option. The defiance option must be written as honestly as the surrender option.

- **Not a spectrum.** The 7 options are not arranged on a continuum from spiritually immature to spiritually mature. They are 7 different authentic responses to the same situation, none of which is inherently closer to God than the others.

- **First person, present tense.** "My jaw locked." "I feel nothing." "Something in my chest softens." Not: "Some people would feel..." or "It would be understandable to..."

- **No interpretive framing.** The option text does not tell the reader what their response means. It only describes the response. The interpretation belongs to the insight.

**Length:** 1-2 sentences. Short enough to feel like a breath, not an explanation.

**Emotional coverage target across 7 options:**

| Slot | Target register |
|---|---|
| 1 | Resistance or physical shutdown (jaw locks, hands grip, body goes rigid) |
| 2 | Shame or exposure (hot face, burning eyes, the urge to disappear) |
| 3 | Numbness or dissociation (going through the motions, sound cutting out) |
| 4 | Rage or defiance (clean anger, white heat, the refusal to accept) |
| 5 | Grief or longing (the tears that arrive without permission, the ache) |
| 6 | Relief or release (unexpected loosening, the exhale that surprises) |
| 7 | Ambiguous or complex (the response that holds two things at once) |

These are targets, not rules. The scenario will determine the actual shape. But if all 7 options cluster in the same emotional register, the reflection is not serving its full range of readers.

---

### 3. The Insights

**What they are:** Pastoral responses to each option — written from the integrated voice of the Wise Spiritual Director and the Wise Pastoral Director (see HOMILETIC_STYLE_GUIDE.md) — that accompany the reader where they are without rushing them somewhere else.

**Governing principles:**

- **Enter before you move.** The first sentence of every insight should be recognizable to the person who chose that option. If the reader chose "My jaw locked," the insight should begin in the jaw. If the reader chose "Something in me softened against my will," the insight should honor the involuntary nature of that softening before it does anything else.

- **Named but not diagnosed.** The insight can identify what is present — the body knows, the defenses are up, the grief arrived before the permission — but it does not reduce the response to a clinical or spiritual category. "The jaw knows before the mind does" is different from "This is cognitive shutdown." Both may be true; only the former accompanies the reader.

- **Theologically anchored, lightly worn.** Every insight should have a theological current running beneath it — but that current does not need to be named. The reader does not need to be told "This is what the text is doing." The connection should be available, not compulsory. The insight should be nourishing to a person who is actively wrestling with faith and to a person who is sitting at the edge of it.

- **No tidy resolution.** The insight should not end with a bow. It can end with a question, an invitation, a Selah, or simply an open door. The reader's process belongs to the reader; the insight does not get to finish it for them.

- **Cadence rotation.** Each insight in a set of 7 should draw from a different primary cadence tool (see HOMILETIC_STYLE_GUIDE.md — The 17 Cadence Tools). If options 3 and 4 both open with Concrete Before Abstract, revise one.

- **Equal length and quality.** The insight for the "easiest" option should not be more developed than the insight for the hardest one. Every reader who showed up deserves the same quality of attention. If you notice yourself writing a longer, richer insight for option 6 (relief) than for option 3 (numbness), redistribute the craft.

**Length:** 4-8 sentences. Long enough to feel like a real response; short enough to feel like presence, not a lecture.

## The Theological Thread

While the scenario is never explicitly theological, each soul reflection is calibrated to the specific chapter's content. The connection should be:

- **Present but unlabeled in the scenario.** The reader can feel the resonance without being told to feel it.
- **Named lightly in 1-2 of the 7 insights.** Not as an interpretive overlay but as a recognition — "what Isaiah calls 'the waters of Shiloah'" or "the prophet's 'be quiet, do not fear.'"
- **Never forced.** If the theological connection would require an awkward reach, the scenario was probably the wrong one for this section. Redesign the scenario rather than straining the insight.

## Anti-Patterns Specific to Soul Reflections

These failures are distinct from those in the Homiletic Style Guide and apply specifically to the soul reflection format:

| Anti-Pattern | What It Does | Fix |
|---|---|---|
| Scenario set in a church context | Activates performance mode before the reader settles in | Ground the scenario in ordinary life |
| Options that are clearly ranked | Sends a message: some responses are more spiritual | Rewrite so no option implies a spiritual hierarchy |
| "If you're in this place, here's how to grow" | Turns the insight into a diagnostic | Remove the growth frame; accompany instead |
| Insight that names what God "wants" the reader to do | Adds a task to what should be a rest | Let the insight observe; let God do the directing |
| 7 options that all cluster in resignation or rage | Fails the full range of readers | Diversify across the emotional vocabulary |
| Insight that begins with "This response shows..." | Labels from above; reduces rather than enters | Start with the body, the room, the sensation |
| Option written as a spiritual question | Moves to the mind too quickly | Stay in the body; save the question for the insight |
| Insight that moralizes in the final sentence | Closes the door with a judgment | End with an open question, an invitation, or silence |
| Scenario that implies a "lesson" before the options | Frames the response before the reader can have it | Revise so the scenario is neutral, not prescriptive |

## Quality Check Before Publishing

Run each completed soul reflection through the following sequence:

**1. The Scenario Test**
Could a thoughtful, faith-adjacent person (not only a committed believer) inhabit this scenario? Does it have a physical location and sensory detail? Is it neutral enough that all 7 options feel plausible?

**2. The Option Spread Test**
Lay the 7 options side by side. Do they span different emotional registers? Could the person who attends your church and the person who attended once and left both find themselves in one of the 7?

**3. The Equal Dignity Test** (Pastoral Director's question)
If someone read all 7 insights, would they conclude that options 1-7 are equally welcomed? Or would one insight feel like a consolation prize?

**4. The Entering Test** (Spiritual Director's question)
Does each insight's first sentence enter the specific experience of the option, or does it move immediately to a reframe? If the reader chose numbness, does the insight honor the numbness before it does anything else?

**5. The Cadence Test**
Read all 7 insights aloud. Do they sound different from each other? Are there two consecutive insights with the same opening strategy? If so, revise.

**6. The Resolution Test**
Read the final sentence of each insight. Does it close the case or leave the door open? Replace any tidy summary with a question, an invitation, or a Selah.

## How This Feature Connects to the Broader App

The Soul Reflection is positioned as the last tab in the Study View, after Verse by Verse, Big Picture, and Framework. This placement is intentional:

- The reader has engaged intellectually and theologically before arriving here
- The soul reflection invites a different mode of engagement — not comprehension but encounter
- The body-first approach reactivates the reader's own experience as the primary text

The soul reflection does not tell the reader what the passage means. It creates conditions for the reader to discover what the passage does — in their body, their history, and their present moment.

## Authorship Note

All soul reflections in this app are written by human authors following the principles above. They draw on the homiletic tradition documented in HOMILETIC_STYLE_GUIDE.md and on the pastoral frameworks of spiritual direction and contemplative care. No soul reflection content is AI-generated.
